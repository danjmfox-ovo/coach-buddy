# Requisite Organisation: Jaques

## Requisite Organisation (Jaques)

**What it is**: A theory of organisational design grounded in a concept of **time-horizon** — the furthest-forward point in time a role must plan and act toward. Jaques argues that the natural hierarchy of any organisation should be structured around distinct strata of time-horizon, each requiring qualitatively different cognitive capability. A requisite (appropriate) organisation is one where role requirements match the cognitive capability of the person in that role.

**Source**: Jaques, E. (1989). *Requisite Organisation*. Cason Hall.
Jaques, E. & Cason, K. (1994). *Human Capability*. Cason Hall.
Jaques, E. (1998). *Requisite Organisation* (2nd ed.). Cason Hall.

---

### Stratum and time-horizon

Jaques identified eight strata of organisational work, differentiated by the time-horizon within which a role operates:

| Stratum | Time-horizon | Typical role |
|---------|------------|-------------|
| S1 | Up to 3 months | Front-line worker |
| S2 | 3 months – 1 year | Team leader / supervisor |
| S3 | 1–2 years | First-line manager |
| S4 | 2–5 years | Business unit manager |
| S5 | 5–10 years | Divisional/subsidiary head |
| S6 | 10–20 years | Group CEO of large organisation |
| S7 | 20–50 years | CEO, multi-national conglomerate |
| S8 | 50+ years | Rare; peak complexity organisations |

**The core claim**: Cognitive capability — the capacity to work with complexity over longer time-horizons — develops in a predictable trajectory over a person's working life and can be measured. Placing a person in a role above their current capability produces dysfunction; placing them significantly below produces frustration and under-contribution.

---

### Cognitive complexity

Jaques used the term **Complexity of Mental Processing** (CMP) to describe the property that differentiates strata. It is not intelligence in the IQ sense, but the capacity to hold multiple variables in mind simultaneously, work with longer cause-effect chains, and reason under genuine uncertainty over extended time periods.

**Four modes of cognitive processing** (summarised):
1. **Declarative** — judgement based on known categories and conditions
2. **Cumulative** — combining several declared items into a new whole
3. **Serial** — sequential conditional logic ("if A, then B, then C")
4. **Parallel** — holding multiple serial paths simultaneously

Higher strata require the higher processing modes.

---

### Accountability and authority alignment

Jaques was insistent that in a healthy organisation:
- Every role has clear accountability — a specific person who can be held responsible
- The authority given to a role must match its accountability — "you cannot hold someone accountable for outcomes they lack the authority to influence"
- A manager must be at least one stratum above their direct reports — if they are at the same stratum, they cannot add value; they become a bottleneck or a redoer

**Coaching application**: When a leader is "doing the work of their reports" — over-hands-on, unable to delegate, solving tactical problems that their team should own — one diagnosis is stratum mismatch. The leader may be at S3 capability in an S4 role, or their reports may be at their level rather than below.

---

### Handle with care: contested and problematic dimensions

**This framework carries significant epistemological and ethical risks. Surface these explicitly if using it with a client.**

**The contested claims**:

1. **Psychometric operationalisation**: Jaques and followers developed instruments (the Career Path Appreciation interview) to assess an individual's current and potential CMP. These tools have not been validated to the standard of peer-reviewed psychometrics. Treating them as objective measures of capability is not warranted by the evidence base.

2. **Biological determinism**: Jaques held that CMP develops on a largely biologically determined trajectory, predictable from an individual's current level. This is a strong nativist claim that reduces human development to unfolding potential rather than cultivated capability. It sits in tension with virtually all developmental psychology, including Vygotsky, Kegan, and the broader constructivist tradition.

3. **Misuse as fixed-ceiling labelling**: The stratum model has been used to tell individuals they will never exceed a certain level. This causes harm, particularly in organisational contexts with existing power asymmetries. A framework designed to improve system fit becomes a tool for limiting individuals.

4. **Hierarchical bias**: The theory is explicitly hierarchical and managerial. It does not engage with heterarchical, networked, or self-organising forms that characterise significant portions of contemporary work. It was developed in and for large, stable, bureaucratic organisations.

---

### What remains useful despite the cautions

- The insight that **role demands differ qualitatively, not just quantitatively** across levels is valuable. A team leader role and a business unit director role are not the same kind of work, more of it — they require different modes of reasoning.
- The **accountability-authority alignment** principle is practically useful and widely applicable.
- The **time-horizon** concept is a productive diagnostic frame: is this person thinking and acting at the right time-horizon for their role? If not, is it a capability question or a structural/incentive question?
- **Stratum mismatch** (role above or below natural level) as a diagnostic category explains some otherwise puzzling patterns — the brilliant specialist who is terrible at managing people; the operational genius who is ineffective at strategy.

---

### When to reach for this

- A leadership team has recurring accountability confusion — decisions made at the wrong level, accountability not matched to authority
- A coach is working with a leader who seems genuinely mismatched with their role's demands — either visibly overwhelmed or visibly under-challenged
- An organisation is redesigning role structures and needs a framework for thinking about level differentiation
- A leader is asking why a particular person "can't think strategically" — worth examining whether the role is designed for strategic thinking or tactical execution

**Use the time-horizon lens and the accountability-authority principle. Be very cautious about assessment tools, capability labelling, or any framing that implies fixed ceilings.**
