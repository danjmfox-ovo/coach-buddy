# Tensions: Polarity Thinking

## Polarity Thinking (Johnson)

**What it is**: A method for identifying and managing ongoing tensions that cannot be resolved — only managed. Distinct from problems, which have solutions.

**Source**: Johnson, B. (1992). *Polarity Management: Identifying and Managing Unsolvable Problems*. HRD Press.

---

### Problems vs Polarities

| | Problem | Polarity |
|-|---------|---------|
| Nature | Has a right answer | Has two right answers in tension |
| Response | Solve it | Manage it |
| Outcome | Done | Ongoing |
| Failure mode | Not solving it | Treating it as a problem to solve |

**Examples of polarities** (not problems):
- Stability ↔ Change
- Individual autonomy ↔ Team alignment
- Short-term delivery ↔ Long-term capability
- Centralised control ↔ Distributed decision-making
- Focus ↔ Flexibility

**The test**: Can you name a genuine upside to both poles? Can you name a downside to over-focusing on either? If yes: polarity, not problem.

---

### The Polarity Map

A polarity is mapped as a two-pole system with four quadrants:

```
              POLE A                    POLE B
         ┌─────────────────┬─────────────────┐
         │   Upsides of A  │   Upsides of B  │
    +    │                 │                 │    +
         ├─────────────────┼─────────────────┤
    -    │  Downsides of A │  Downsides of B │    -
         └─────────────────┴─────────────────┘
```

The system oscillates between poles. Over-investment in one pole produces the downsides of that pole, which creates pressure to swing toward the other. The goal is not to find the midpoint — it is to get the upsides of both over time.

**Action steps and early warnings**: Good polarity work identifies:
- Action steps to leverage the upsides of each pole
- Early warnings (signals that you've over-invested in one pole and need to rebalance)

---

### When to reach for this

- A coaching client is experiencing a recurring conflict that never resolves
- A team keeps oscillating between two failure modes ("we're too centralised" → "we're too chaotic")
- A strategic debate where both sides are genuinely right
- A leader is forcing a solution to something that keeps coming back
- The word "balance" keeps appearing — balance is often a polarity management problem

**The reframe**: "This isn't a problem to solve — it's a tension to manage. Both poles have real upsides. The question is: how do we get the upsides of both over time, and what are the early warnings that we've over-invested in one?"

---

### Common coaching error

Treating polarities as problems produces: choosing a pole, over-committing to it, experiencing the downsides, then swinging to the other pole and repeating. This looks like "we tried that and it didn't work" — but the failure was not the pole, it was the over-investment.

---

### Polarity thinking and Cynefin

In Complex domain situations (Cynefin): many apparent problems are actually polarities that emerge from the domain's nature — contradictions don't resolve, they need to be held. Polarity thinking is one way of working in the Complex domain without flattening it.
