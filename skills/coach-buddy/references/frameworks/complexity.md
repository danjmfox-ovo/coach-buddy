# Complexity: Cynefin and Related

## Cynefin (Snowden)

**What it is**: A sense-making framework that distinguishes five domains based on the relationship between cause and effect.

| Domain | Cause-effect relationship | Right response |
|--------|--------------------------|----------------|
| Clear (Simple) | Obvious, predictable | Sense → Categorise → Respond (best practice) |
| Complicated | Knowable with expertise | Sense → Analyse → Respond (good practice) |
| Complex | Only visible in retrospect | Probe → Sense → Respond (safe-to-fail experiments) |
| Chaotic | No visible relationship | Act → Sense → Respond (stabilise first) |
| Confused / Aporetic | Domain is unclear | Decompose into sub-problems; treat disorder as a signal |

**The boundary that matters most in coaching**: Complicated vs Complex.
- Complicated: expert analysis will find the answer. Bring in the right people.
- Complex: no expert has the answer in advance. Experiment, learn, adapt.

Imposing complicated responses (best practice, standards, frameworks) on complex situations is a common failure mode. Coaches who flatten complexity to "we just need a better process" are usually here.

**Disorder (centre)**: When you can't tell which domain you're in, resist the pull to categorise. Sit with the uncertainty, gather more signal.

**When to reach for this**:
- Coach is proposing a solution that assumes more predictability than the situation warrants
- Team is in disagreement about whether there IS a right answer
- Coach is experiencing high certainty in a situation that is clearly shifting or ambiguous
- "Safe-to-fail experiment" is the intervention under consideration

**Common misreads**:
- "Complex" does not mean "complicated and hard." It means cause-and-effect are not predictable in advance.
- The Clear domain is not always safe — it can become Chaotic if assumptions are disrupted suddenly (the boundary is fragile).
- Cynefin is a sense-making tool, not a classification system. You can move between domains.

**Source**: Snowden, D. & Boone, M. (2007). "A Leader's Framework for Decision Making." Harvard Business Review.

## Safe-to-Fail Experiments

When in Complex: design small experiments with these properties:
- **Reversible**: can be stopped or undone if it amplifies the wrong thing
- **Small-scale**: limited blast radius
- **Multiple simultaneous**: run several probes; amplify what works, dampen what doesn't
- **Learning-oriented**: the point is information, not delivery

The contrast with a pilot: a pilot tests whether a known solution scales. A safe-to-fail experiment tests whether an approach is viable at all.

## Related lens: Simple/Complicated/Complex/Chaotic in team contexts

| Signal | Likely domain |
|--------|--------------|
| Team follows a clear process and it reliably works | Clear |
| Team has recurring problems that experts can diagnose | Complicated |
| Team keeps trying solutions that don't stick; situation keeps shifting | Complex |
| Acute crisis; normal operations have broken down | Chaotic |
| Team is arguing about which of the above they're in | Confused |
