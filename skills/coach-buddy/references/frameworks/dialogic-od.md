# Dialogic OD: Bushe and Marshak

## Dialogic Organisation Development (Bushe, Marshak)

**What it is**: A family of OD approaches that work through changing the conversations, stories, and generative images that shape an organisation's reality — rather than changing structures and processes directly. Contrasts with Diagnostic OD, which assumes a problem exists, can be analysed, and can be solved by an expert.

**Source**: Bushe, G. & Marshak, R. (eds.) (2015). *Dialogic Organisation Development: The Theory and Practice of Transformational Change*. Berrett-Koehler.
Marshak, R. & Grant, D. (2008). "Organisational Discourse and New OD Paradigms." *Journal of Applied Behavioral Science*.

---

### Diagnostic vs Dialogic OD

| Dimension | Diagnostic OD | Dialogic OD |
|-----------|-------------|------------|
| Assumption about reality | Objective; can be known and measured | Socially constructed through conversation and meaning |
| Role of consultant/coach | Expert who analyses and recommends | Container-holder who enables new conversations |
| How change happens | Implementing solutions to diagnosed problems | Destabilising existing narratives; creating space for new ones |
| Data | Surveys, interviews, observation | Stories, conversations, competing narratives |
| Change model | Lewin's unfreeze-change-refreeze | Generative image; continuous inquiry |
| Primary methods | Organisation survey, action research, process consultation | Appreciative Inquiry, Open Space, World Café, Future Search |

**Neither approach is always right.** Diagnostic OD is appropriate when a definable problem exists and needs expert intervention. Dialogic OD is appropriate when the problem is that people are stuck in a story that constrains possibilities, or when the desired change is not a solution but a transformation of how people see the situation.

---

### Generative images

A central concept in Dialogic OD. A **generative image** is a phrase, metaphor, or story that disrupts old thinking and opens new possibility. It is not an insight or analysis — it is something that changes what questions people ask and what actions feel available.

**Examples**:
- "We are not a company with a digital strategy; we are a digital company" (changes identity narrative)
- "What if we treated users as partners in design rather than recipients of decisions?"
- Appreciative Inquiry's "positive core" — shifting from "what's broken?" to "what gives life to this organisation at its best?"

**Coaching application**: When a team or leader is stuck, one question is: what is the generative image they need? Not: what is the solution they need. The right image can shift what is possible more than the right recommendation.

---

### Appreciative Inquiry (Cooperrider, Whitney)

The most widely used Dialogic OD method. Organises inquiry around what works — peak experiences, strengths, vitality — rather than problems and deficits.

**4-D Cycle**:
1. **Discover** — What works? When are we at our best?
2. **Dream** — What could we be if we built on this?
3**Design** — What structures, processes, and agreements support the dream?
4. **Destiny/Deploy** — What actions do we commit to?

**Coaching application**: Particularly useful when a team or organisation has become deficit-focused and the problem-talk is depleting energy rather than generating solutions. Also useful with leaders who are strong critics but have lost contact with what they value.

**The caution**: AI is not a panacea for avoiding difficult realities. When used as a way to bypass conflict, real problems, or legitimate grievance, it produces change theatre. The inquiry must be genuinely open — not steered toward a predetermined positive narrative.

---

### Large-group methods and containers

Dialogic OD is associated with large-group methods designed to bring "the whole system into the room":

| Method | Purpose |
|--------|---------|
| **Open Space Technology** (Owen) | Self-organising conference around a complex question; no fixed agenda |
| **World Café** | Small-group dialogue on a shared question; tables rotate to cross-pollinate |
| **Future Search** (Weisbord, Janoff) | Whole-system conference to find common ground and action |

**What these share**: The designer does not know in advance what will emerge. The role is to create conditions for generative dialogue, not to guide the group to a predetermined conclusion.

---

### When to reach for this

- Diagnostic approaches have been tried (surveys, action plans, process changes) and the organisation is still stuck in the same place
- The presenting problem is about meaning, narrative, or identity rather than structure or process
- There is a need for genuine, broad-based participation in a change — not consultation that feeds into a decision someone else will make
- A team keeps analysing problems rather than generating movement; inquiry energy is exhausted
- A coach is being asked to be "the expert with the answer" when the situation actually calls for the organisation to find its own answer

---

### Epistemological note

Dialogic OD rests on a constructionist epistemology: organisations are not pre-existing objects that can be accurately diagnosed, but ongoing social constructions produced by the conversations people have. This is a significant departure from most management consulting and some coaching traditions. Practitioners who hold a realist epistemology (there is a right answer, and expertise means knowing it) will find Dialogic OD uncomfortable — and may, if they impose their epistemology, undermine the approach. Knowing which epistemology you're working from is more important than choosing the right method.
