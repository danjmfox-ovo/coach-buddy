# Viable System Model: Beer

## Viable System Model (Beer)

**What it is**: A model of the systems and functions any organisation needs to remain viable — capable of independent existence — in a changing environment. Grounded in cybernetics. Five systems, recursive: each viable unit (a team, a department, an organisation) contains the same structure.

**Source**: Beer, S. (1972). *Brain of the Firm*. Allen Lane.
Beer, S. (1979). *The Heart of Enterprise*. Wiley.
Beer, S. (1985). *Diagnosing the Organisation*. Wiley.

---

### The five systems

| System | Function | Analogous to |
|--------|---------|-------------|
| **S1** | Primary activities — the work that creates value | Operational units |
| **S2** | Coordination — preventing S1 units from interfering with each other | Scheduling, standards, shared services |
| **S3** | Control — managing the internal environment; resource allocation and performance | Operational management |
| **S3*** | Audit — direct sporadic access to S1 to verify what S3 reports | Internal audit, skip-level conversations |
| **S4** | Intelligence — scanning the external environment; future adaptation | Strategy, market analysis, R&D |
| **S5** | Policy — defining identity, values, and the balance between S3 and S4 | Board, executive, governing principles |

**The key structural insight**: S3 manages today; S4 manages tomorrow; S5 holds the balance between them. An organisation that has strong S3 and weak S4 is efficient today but will not adapt. An organisation with strong S4 and weak S3 is strategically sophisticated but operationally chaotic.

---

### Recursion

The VSM is recursive — the same five-system structure appears at every level. A team is a viable system within a department which is a viable system within a division. This means:

- The model can be applied at any level of granularity
- Pathologies at one level (e.g. a team's coordination failures) are often symptoms of dysfunction at a higher level (e.g. the organisation's S2 is so centralised that teams cannot self-regulate)

---

### Variety and the Law of Requisite Variety

Beer draws on Ashby's **Law of Requisite Variety**: to control a system, a manager or regulatory function must have variety (options, responses) at least equal to the variety of the system being managed.

**The practical meaning**: A manager who has fewer response options than the complexity of the team's situation will default to simplifying the situation rather than responding to it. This is how over-standardisation, premature process, and micromanagement emerge — not from malice but from variety mismatch.

**Coaching application**: When a leader is over-controlling, ask: what variety do they lack? What options are missing from their repertoire? The pathology is usually a variety problem, not a motivation problem.

---

### Algedonic channel

Beer's term for the pain/pleasure signal that bypasses normal management channels and goes directly to the top when survival is threatened. In healthy organisations, this channel is open — urgent signals reach the people who can act on them. In unhealthy organisations, it is blocked by hierarchy, culture, or management layers.

**Coaching use**: When a serious problem was known at the front line but never reached leadership until it was a crisis — the algedonic channel is blocked. This is a structural problem, not a communication problem.

---

### Diagnosing with the VSM

Common pathologies the model surfaces:

| Signal | VSM diagnosis |
|--------|-------------|
| Teams constantly fighting over resources or priorities | S2 is weak or absent |
| Operational management overwhelmed; no capacity for future thinking | S3 is consuming all attention; S4 is starved |
| Strategy keeps changing without embedding | S4–S3 connection is broken; S5 is not holding the balance |
| Front-line problems invisible to leadership until they're crises | Algedonic channel is blocked; S3* is absent |
| Units optimising locally in ways that harm the whole | S2 coordination mechanisms are missing or toothless |

---

### When to reach for this

- An organisation or team is experiencing recurring coordination failures that process interventions haven't fixed
- A leader is asking "why doesn't our strategy actually change anything?" — likely a broken S4→S3 connection
- A coach is working with a system that seems too complex to map with simpler models
- There is a recurring pattern of "we didn't know until it was too late"
- A team is arguing about who owns coordination — VSM names the coordination function explicitly

---

### Caveats for coaching use

The VSM is a diagnostic tool. It does not prescribe organisational structure — it identifies which functions are present, absent, or misaligned. Beer was explicit that the model describes what any viable system must do, not how it should be structured. A pair of colleagues over coffee can perform all five functions; so can a 50,000-person organisation.

The model has a steep learning curve and can feel mechanistic. It is most useful when simpler lenses (team health, communication patterns) have been tried and the problem persists. Introduce it as a question-generating frame, not a diagnosis to hand over.
