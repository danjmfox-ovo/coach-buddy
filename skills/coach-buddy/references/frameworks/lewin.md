# Field Theory and Force Field Analysis: Lewin

## Field Theory (Lewin)

**What it is**: A psychological theory holding that behaviour is a function of the person and their psychological environment (the "field") at any given moment. B = f(P, E). The field is the totality of coexisting facts, conceived as mutually interdependent. You cannot understand a person's behaviour by looking at either the person or the environment in isolation.

**Source**: Lewin, K. (1936). *Principles of Topological Psychology*. McGraw-Hill.
Lewin, K. (1951). *Field Theory in Social Science*. Harper & Row.
Lewin, K. (1947). "Frontiers in Group Dynamics." *Human Relations*.

---

### The field concept

Lewin drew the field concept from physics but applied it psychologically: the field is not the physical environment but the **life space** — the environment as the person experiences it. Two people in the same room are in different fields if they experience the situation differently.

**Coaching implication**: To understand why someone is acting as they are, understand the field as they experience it — not as it objectively is. "Resistance to change" is usually entirely rational given the field the person is in. The question is not "why are they resistant?" but "what does the field look like from where they stand?"

---

### Life space, barriers, and valence

Key concepts from field theory:

| Concept | Meaning |
|---------|---------|
| **Life space** | The totality of facts that determine behaviour for a given person at a given moment |
| **Valence** | The attractiveness (positive valence) or repellence (negative valence) of goals and objects in the field |
| **Tension** | A state of disequilibrium that motivates movement toward a goal |
| **Barriers** | Constraints that block movement through the life space toward a goal |
| **Locomotion** | Movement through the life space — achieving goals, changing position |

**The leadership application**: A change initiative that looks obvious from the outside may have high negative valence in the field of those affected. Understanding the specific barriers and negative valences — not just assuming they are irrational — is the prerequisite for any effective change work.

## Force Field Analysis (Lewin)

**What it is**: A practical tool derived from field theory. Any situation can be understood as a dynamic equilibrium between forces driving change (driving forces) and forces resisting change (restraining forces). The current state persists because these forces are in balance.

**Source**: Lewin, K. (1943). "Defining the 'Field at a Given Time.'" *Psychological Review*.
Lewin, K. (1947). "Quasi-Stationary Social Equilibria and the Problem of Permanent Change." In Newcomb & Hartley (eds.), *Readings in Social Psychology*.

---

### The core structure

```
                    ← Desired state
Current state ──────────────────────────────
                    Forces restraining
────────────────────────────────────────────
                    Forces driving →
```

A situation stays where it is because driving and restraining forces are in equilibrium. To move toward the desired state:

1. Strengthen driving forces
2. Reduce restraining forces
3. Both

**The counterintuitive finding**: Lewin's research suggested that **reducing restraining forces** is generally more effective than **increasing driving forces**. Increasing pressure in the face of restraint produces tension and can generate new resistance. Removing barriers is often lower energy and more durable.

---

### Conducting a force field analysis

A practical sequence:

1. **Define the desired state clearly** — vague goal = vague analysis
2. **Name the driving forces** — people, pressures, incentives, aspirations that are already pushing toward change
3. **Name the restraining forces** — habits, fears, structural barriers, competing commitments, skill gaps
4. **Assess magnitude and manageability** — which forces are strongest? Which are within the team's influence?
5. **Design interventions** — primarily to reduce restraining forces; secondarily to amplify drivers
6. **Identify early signals** — how will you know if the balance is shifting?

---

### Unfreeze–Change–Refreeze

Lewin's three-phase model of change is frequently attributed to him, though the precise formulation is largely a later reconstruction (Coch & French drew on his ideas; the tidy three-phrase model was codified posthumously):

1. **Unfreeze** — disrupt the current equilibrium; create the conditions for change
2. **Change** — move to the new state
3. **Refreeze** — stabilise the new state so it becomes the new equilibrium

**The coaching use**: This model is too linear for most complex change. Useful as a diagnostic: Is the problem that the system hasn't been unfrozen (people don't see why change is needed)? That the change phase is failing (the new behaviour isn't embedding)? That refreezing is absent (the change happens, then the old patterns reassert)?

**The caution**: "Refreeze" is increasingly contested as a concept — in turbulent environments, the goal is not a new equilibrium but ongoing adaptability. The unfreeze-change-refreeze model was developed for manufacturing and stable organisations; apply it to complex adaptive systems carefully.

---

### Action research

Lewin also originated the **action research** cycle: a spiral of planning, acting, observing, and reflecting. The practitioner is not a neutral observer but a participant whose interventions shape the field being studied.

**For coaches**: This positions the coach as part of the field — not outside it. The coach's presence, questions, and framing change what is possible in the system. This is sometimes called a "systemic" stance; its roots are here.

---

### When to reach for this

- A change initiative is stalling — force field analysis surfaces specific restraining forces rather than vague "resistance to change"
- A team is stuck in a pattern and increasing pressure (more process, more accountability) is not working — examine restraining forces
- A leader is pushing harder and harder for a change with diminishing returns — likely amplifying resistance by focusing on drivers
- A coach wants to surface the difference between what is driving and what is restraining within one person (force field analysis can be applied intrapersonally)
- A situation appears static but should be changing — what are the forces maintaining the equilibrium?
