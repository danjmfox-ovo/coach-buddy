# Work Layers: Shorrock's Four Work Layers

## The Framework (Shorrock)

Four distinct perspectives on how work happens — each valid, each incomplete on its own:

| Layer | Name | What it captures |
|-------|------|-----------------|
| 1 | **Work-as-Imagined** | How decision-makers, designers, and leaders believe work is done |
| 2 | **Work-as-Prescribed** | Formal rules, procedures, standards, guidelines |
| 3 | **Work-as-Disclosed** | What workers say they do (in interviews, audits, retrospectives) |
| 4 | **Work-as-Done** | What actually happens at the sharp end |

**The gap that matters**: Work-as-Imagined / Work-as-Prescribed is almost always different from Work-as-Done. This gap is not a failure — it is inevitable. Humans adapt to conditions. The question is whether the adaptation is safe, sustainable, and visible.

**Source**: Shorrock, S. (2016). "The varieties of human work." Humanistic Systems blog. Also developed in: Hollnagel, E. et al., *Resilience Engineering in Practice*.

---

## When to reach for this

- Coach is reporting that "people aren't following the process"
- A retrospective produced action items that didn't change anything
- A team has documentation/guidelines that nobody reads
- Leadership is frustrated that a change initiative isn't landing
- There's a gap between what is said in meetings and what happens in practice

The lens reframes "people aren't following the process" as: *which* process? The prescribed one, or the one that actually enables delivery? Both are real.

---

## Diagnostic questions per layer

**Work-as-Imagined**: What does leadership believe happens? Have they watched it? When did they last work at the sharp end?

**Work-as-Prescribed**: What do the formal rules say? Are they current? Were they written by people who understand the work?

**Work-as-Disclosed**: What do practitioners say in retrospectives, audits, stand-ups? What are they comfortable surfacing vs. what stays private?

**Work-as-Done**: What would you see if you sat beside someone doing this work? Where do they deviate from the prescribed process, and why? What adaptations are invisible?

---

## Common coaching applications

**Process adoption failures**: The prescribed process (layer 2) was designed against an imagined version of work (layer 1) that doesn't match reality (layer 4). Compliance theatre results — people follow the form without the substance.

**Retro action items that evaporate**: The retro captures work-as-disclosed (layer 3), which may not match work-as-done (layer 4). The actions address the disclosed problem, not the real one.

**"We have a culture problem"**: Often: work-as-imagined (the culture we want) vs. work-as-done (the culture we have). The gap is the culture problem. Name the layers, not just the gap.

---

## Relationship to Safety-II

Shorrock's framework is closely related to Safety-II thinking (Hollnagel): work-as-done contains the adaptations and expertise that make things go right most of the time. Pathologising the gap (treating deviations as violations) destroys the very intelligence that keeps systems stable.
