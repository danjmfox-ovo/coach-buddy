# Therapeutic Lenses in Coaching

## Responsible Borrowing Frame

Coaches working in organisational contexts regularly encounter concepts that originate in psychotherapy. This is normal, productive, and sometimes necessary — many of the most useful coaching concepts (psychological safety, meaning-making, parts work) would not exist without their therapeutic roots.

The caution is not about borrowing. It is about:

1. **Scope**: Therapeutic lenses illuminate patterns; they do not make coaches therapists. When a client needs therapeutic support, name it and refer.
2. **Epistemology**: Some therapeutic approaches carry strong claims about how the psyche works. Hold these as hypotheses, not as maps of reality.
3. **Power**: Therapy involves a power differential. Coaching involves one too. Concepts developed for high-frequency, long-term therapeutic relationships do not always translate cleanly to brief, goal-oriented, organisationally-embedded coaching.
4. **The certainty problem**: Approaches that give the facilitator or coach privileged access to what is "really" happening for the client — especially in group work — warrant scrutiny. The risk increases in polarised or hierarchical contexts, where the appearance of revealed truth can be weaponised.

## Internal Family Systems (Schwartz)

**What it is**: A model of the mind as comprising multiple "parts" — each with its own perspective, feelings, and motivation — alongside a "Self" that is the seat of clarity, compassion, and presence. Parts develop in response to experience; some protect by managing, some protect by containing (exiles). When the Self leads, parts are not running the show.

**Source**: Schwartz, R. (1995). *Internal Family Systems Therapy*. Guilford Press.
Schwartz, R. (2021). *No Bad Parts: Healing Trauma and Restoring Wholeness with the Internal Family Systems Model*. Sounds True.

---

### Core concepts

| Concept | Description |
|---------|------------|
| **Self** | A quality of presence — curious, calm, connected, compassionate, confident, creative, courageous, clear. Not a part; the quality that emerges when parts are not blended or in control. |
| **Parts** | Distinct voices, states, or perspectives within the system — not metaphors, but functional presences |
| **Managers** | Proactive protectors — the inner critics, perfectionist parts, high-control parts that try to prevent pain |
| **Firefighters** | Reactive protectors — parts that respond to triggered pain with distraction, avoidance, or impulsive behaviour |
| **Exiles** | Parts carrying pain, fear, or shame that the system works hard to keep contained |
| **Blending** | When a part's state floods Self — you are not observing the part, you have become it |

---

### Coaching application

IFS concepts are useful in coaching without requiring formal IFS practice. The core moves:

- **Naming the inner critic as a part**: "It sounds like there's a part of you that says you'll fail. What is that part trying to protect?" This depersonalises the self-attack without minimising it.
- **Noticing blending**: When a client is fully in a reactive state and cannot get perspective — "It sounds like you're completely in that right now. Is there any part of you that can observe it?" This invites Self-energy.
- **Acknowledging competing parts as real**: "It sounds like one part of you wants to change this and another part is not so sure. Both are doing something for you." This avoids the trap of siding with the conscious preference against the "resistant" part.

**The limit**: IFS has a full therapeutic protocol (unburdening, direct access, unblending with exiles) that belongs in therapy, not coaching. The concepts travel; the protocol doesn't.

## Gestalt

**What it is**: A therapeutic approach — developed by Fritz and Laura Perls — grounded in the primacy of present-moment awareness, the self as a process of contact with the environment, and the belief that growth happens through completing unfinished patterns (gestalts). Widely influential in coaching, OD, and group facilitation.

**Source**: Perls, F., Hefferline, R. & Goodman, P. (1951). *Gestalt Therapy: Excitement and Growth in the Human Personality*. Julian Press.
Zinker, J. (1977). *Creative Process in Gestalt Therapy*. Brunner/Mazel.

---

### Key concepts

| Concept | Description |
|---------|------------|
| **Contact** | Full engagement with what is present — a person, an idea, a feeling. Contact precedes learning. |
| **Contact boundary** | Where self and environment meet. Disturbances here are where problems live. |
| **Figure and ground** | What comes into awareness (figure) against the background of everything else (ground). What becomes figure reveals what matters. |
| **Unfinished business** | Incomplete experiences that pull attention and energy. A recurring pattern is often an unfinished gestalt seeking completion. |
| **Awareness** | The foundational Gestalt principle: full awareness itself is therapeutic. What is fully seen does not need to be pushed. |
| **Experiment** | A Gestalt coach proposes an experiment — trying something out in the moment — rather than giving advice. |

---

### Coaching application

Gestalt is particularly relevant for:

- **Here-and-now awareness**: "What are you noticing right now?" The Gestalt move is toward what is present, not toward analysis of what happened.
- **Contact and withdrawal in teams**: Teams that avoid conflict are withdrawing from contact. Teams that only engage in surface politeness are not making real contact. Naming contact quality is useful.
- **Experiments in session**: "Would you be willing to try something? Say that again, but this time stand up." The experiment creates data from direct experience rather than analysis.
- **The body as figure**: Gestalt attends to how the body reflects what the person is holding. Noticing and naming this (with explicit permission) surfaces what words haven't.

**The limit**: Empty Chair and other Gestalt techniques require training and clinical judgement. The phenomenological stance and present-moment awareness are broadly applicable; the intensive techniques are not.

## Attachment Theory (Bowlby, Ainsworth)

**What it is**: A theory of how early relational experiences shape the internal working models people carry into adult relationships. Secure attachment develops when caregivers are reliably responsive; insecure patterns (anxious, avoidant, disorganised) develop in response to inconsistent, unavailable, or frightening caregiving. These patterns — particularly relevant under stress — activate in adult relationships, including with managers, teams, and organisations.

**Source**: Bowlby, J. (1969, 1973, 1980). *Attachment and Loss* (3 vols). Basic Books.
Ainsworth, M. et al. (1978). *Patterns of Attachment*. Erlbaum.
Siegel, D. (1999). *The Developing Mind*. Guilford Press.

---

### Attachment patterns in adult contexts

| Style | Internal model | Behaviour under stress |
|-------|--------------|----------------------|
| **Secure** | "I am worthy of care; others can be relied on" | Can seek support; tolerates uncertainty; recovers |
| **Anxious / preoccupied** | "I'm not sure I'm valued; I need to check" | Hypervigilance to relationship signals; over-reassurance-seeking; conflict amplification |
| **Avoidant / dismissing** | "I don't need others; I manage alone" | Suppresses need; withdraws under pressure; undervalues relationship and support |
| **Disorganised / unresolved** | Contradictory; no coherent strategy | Unpredictable; can appear both seeking and repelling closeness |

---

### Coaching application

**For leaders**:
- A leader's attachment style shapes their availability to their team under stress. An avoidant leader will withdraw precisely when the team needs most presence.
- A leader with anxious attachment may seek excessive reassurance from their team or become destabilised by any hint of dissatisfaction.
- The concept of **earned security** — people can develop more secure functioning through subsequent relationships and reflective practice — is useful for coaching. Attachment is not fixed fate.

**For teams**:
- The team as an attachment figure: teams provide or fail to provide a secure base for risk-taking. A team where "it's safe to fail" is providing the relational conditions for exploration.
- Organisational change as attachment disruption: restructures, leadership changes, and redundancies activate attachment systems. Responses that look like "resistance" or "grief" are often attachment responses to loss of relational context.

**For coaches**:
- The coaching relationship itself has an attachment quality. Clients with avoidant patterns may resist dependency on the coach. Clients with anxious patterns may seek reassurance rather than learning.

**The limit**: Assigning attachment "diagnoses" to leaders is not coaching — it is amateur therapy. The useful move is noticing patterns under stress and exploring what is being activated, not labelling the person.

## Field Theory in Therapeutic Contexts (Lewin, gestalt extensions)

Lewin's field theory (see also `lewin.md`) was extended into therapeutic and group work contexts — particularly through Gestalt and later by theorists including Parlett and the phenomenological field perspective.

**The key moves for coaching and group work**:

- The field is always co-created: what a client presents is not just "theirs" — it is shaped by the relational field in the room. The coach is part of the field.
- The present field contains the past: historical patterns, unfinished business, and relational dynamics are alive in the present moment, not only in explicit narrative.
- **Phenomenological stance**: Take what is given in the field seriously as data, without rushing to interpret or analyse. What is here? What wants attention?

**Application**: This becomes useful in group or team facilitation — noticing what energy, emotion, or theme keeps recurring in a group as data about the field, not just about individual members. "I'm noticing we keep returning to the same impasse. What is this group holding that hasn't been said yet?"

## Constellation Work

**What it is**: A facilitation method — most associated with Bert Hellinger's Family Constellations — in which elements of a system (family members, organisational roles, forces) are spatially represented using physical objects or people as representatives. The positions and movements of representatives are treated as revealing hidden systemic dynamics.

**Sources**: Hellinger, B. (1998). *Love's Hidden Symmetry*. Zeig Tucker.
Varga von Kibéd, M. & Sparrer, I. — SySt (Systemic Structural Constellations), an independently developed variant.

---

### What is useful

- **Spatial externalisation**: Representing a system physically — using people, chairs, or objects — creates a different kind of visibility than verbal description alone. Teams can "see" their system from outside it.
- **Field insight**: Watching how representatives spontaneously move or where they want to stand can surface information that abstract analysis missed. The "field" speaks through the body.
- **Systemic perspective**: The method foregrounds relationships, roles, and systemic forces rather than individual psychology. This is often exactly what an organisationally-embedded coach needs.

---

### What warrants caution

**Hellinger's methodology carries significant problems that practitioners should name explicitly rather than inherit silently:**

**Authoritarian epistemology**: Hellinger claimed privileged access to the "truth" of family systems — he would declare what had "happened" or what was "right" in a system based on his reading of the constellation. This is a facilitator-held truth model. It is not compatible with coaching ethics, client self-determination, or epistemological humility.

**Problematic history**: Hellinger's later work included explicit statements on gender roles, domestic violence, and perpetrator-victim dynamics that are widely considered harmful. These are not peripheral; they reflect the underlying epistemology of the method.

**Suggestibility**: Representatives in constellation work are highly susceptible to suggestion — from the facilitator's framing, from group norms, from the spatial setup. Treating representative movements as direct access to systemic truth is a methodological confusion.

**Particularly risky in polarised or hierarchical contexts**: Approaches that offer revealed truth through a trusted facilitator are especially dangerous when authority is contested or power asymmetries are present. The method can reinforce rather than surface dynamics.

---

### What to do with this

Use **spatial methods and systemic externalisation** as tools that generate useful hypotheses — not truths. "Representatives often move in ways that surprise them — what might that be showing us about this system?" is a very different stance from "this is what your organisation is holding."

Practitioners who use constellation-adjacent methods should:
- Distinguish their practice clearly from Hellinger's methodology
- Maintain an explicitly hypothetical framing — constellations generate questions, not answers
- Be especially alert to moments when the group (or they themselves) treat a representative movement as definitive
- Be cautious in organisational settings where hierarchy means some voices will dominate interpretation

**The SySt (Systemic Structural Constellations)** approach developed by Varga von Kibéd and Sparrer has attempted to create a more methodologically rigorous and epistemologically humble variant. It is worth knowing as an alternative lineage.

## General caution: certainty and facilitator-held truth

These approaches vary widely in their relationship to facilitator authority. A pattern worth watching across all of them: **approaches that give the coach or facilitator privileged access to what is "really" happening for the client.**

In polarised or destabilised organisational climates, this pattern is especially potent. A coach or facilitator who speaks from a position of epistemological certainty — "what this really shows is..." — is exercising significant power. The question is whether that power is being exercised with awareness and consent, or whether it is naturalised as "the method."

The coaching stance that handles this most robustly: the coach observes, names, and offers hypotheses; the client/group tests, contests, and owns the meaning. The coach is a witness and a companion in inquiry — not a reader of hidden truth.
