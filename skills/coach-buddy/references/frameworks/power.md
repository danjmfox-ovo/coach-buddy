# Power: French & Raven and Lukes

## Social Bases of Power (French, Raven)

**What it is**: A taxonomy of the bases from which interpersonal power is exercised in organisations. Originally descriptive — a map of how power operates in social influence situations — and widely used as a coaching diagnostic.

**Source**: French, J.R.P. & Raven, B. (1959). "The Bases of Social Power." In Cartwright, D. (ed.), *Studies in Social Power*. University of Michigan Press.
Raven, B. (1965, 1993). Later revisions added informational power and elaborated the original six.

---

### The six bases

| Base | Source of power | Characteristics |
|------|----------------|----------------|
| **Legitimate** | Formal position; the right to direct because of role | Bounded by role scope; can be challenged on procedural grounds |
| **Reward** | Ability to give something the other person values | Erodes if rewards feel insufficient or contingent; creates compliance, not commitment |
| **Coercive** | Ability to punish, withhold, or harm | Produces compliance under observation; damages trust; creates active resistance over time |
| **Expert** | Knowledge, skill, or experience the other person values | Effective while expertise is perceived as relevant; vulnerable to being bypassed |
| **Referent** | Identification, admiration, trust — the other person wants to be associated with you | Durable; builds genuine commitment; hardest to develop, easiest to lose |
| **Informational** | Control of information needed by the other person | Related to expert power but focused on access rather than knowledge |

**The diagnostic question**: Which bases are a leader actually using, and which bases do they believe they are using? Coaches often encounter leaders who believe they are using expert or referent power while team members experience coercive or reward power. The gap between intended and received power dynamics is frequently the coaching problem.

---

### Dynamics and combinations

- **Coercive and reward power** are positional — they depend on structural role. When the role changes, they disappear.
- **Expert and referent power** are personal — they travel with the individual. A leader who has built genuine expertise and trust retains influence regardless of title.
- **Legitimate power** is positional but psychologically complex: it depends on the target accepting the legitimacy of the structure. In low-trust or contested organisational contexts, legitimate power may not operate as expected.

**The shift that coaching often enables**: Leaders who over-rely on coercive or reward power (because they are easy and immediate) and underinvest in expert and referent power (because they require sustained investment). The long-run costs of the former and benefits of the latter become visible when the coach surfaces the pattern.

## Three Dimensions of Power (Lukes)

**What it is**: A political theory framework distinguishing three increasingly less visible dimensions of power. Directly useful for coaching in contexts involving systemic or structural issues — diversity and inclusion, organisational politics, silencing, change resistance.

**Source**: Lukes, S. (1974). *Power: A Radical View*. Macmillan.
Lukes, S. (2005). *Power: A Radical View* (2nd ed.). Palgrave Macmillan.

---

### The three dimensions

| Dimension | What it looks like | What it takes to see it |
|-----------|-------------------|------------------------|
| **First dimension** | Visible conflict; A wins over B in an observable decision | Just watch who wins |
| **Second dimension** | Non-decision-making; shaping the agenda so certain issues never come up | Looking for what is absent, not what is present |
| **Third dimension** | Shaping preferences; power works by influencing what people want, believe, or think is possible | Questioning common sense, naturalised assumptions |

**First dimension**: Power is A making B do something B wouldn't otherwise do. Observable, analysable, contestable.

**Second dimension** (Bachrach, Baratz): Power also operates in agenda-setting — who controls which issues are legitimately raised. The powerful do not just win in conflicts; they prevent conflicts from arising. "We don't do things that way here" without explanation.

**Third dimension** (Lukes's distinctive contribution): The most insidious form. Power shapes the consciousness of subordinate groups so that they do not perceive their interests clearly, do not experience deprivation as deprivation, or accept the existing order as natural and inevitable. Consent is manufactured, not given freely.

**The coaching implication**: Many "culture" and "engagement" problems operate in the second and third dimensions. Asking "why don't people speak up?" may be a first-dimension question. The second-dimension question is "what is off the table before anyone sits down?" The third-dimension question is "what is the shared assumption so deep that people cannot articulate it as an assumption?"

---

### Positional and relational power

Combining French/Raven with Lukes gives a more complete picture:

- French/Raven describes **how** power is exercised in interpersonal relationships
- Lukes describes **where** power operates structurally and ideologically

A coach who only uses French/Raven will miss systemic power. A coach who only uses Lukes may lack practical interpersonal diagnostics.

---

### When to reach for this

- A leader is frustrated that "nobody challenges me" — examine which dimension of power makes challenge feel unsafe or impossible
- A D&I initiative is producing compliance behaviours without genuine culture change — third-dimension power shaping what is considered normal
- A change initiative is failing for reasons nobody can articulate — look for second-dimension power (agenda-setting that excludes the real issue)
- A team member is not using influence they nominally have — which bases are available to them? Which feel illegitimate in this context?
- A coach is working with someone in a positional power asymmetry — name the bases explicitly

---

### Important caution

Power analysis, especially Lukes's third dimension, can produce a totalising reading of situations — "everything is power." This is not useful for coaching. Use power frames to illuminate what other lenses miss; do not substitute them for attention to individuals' agency, capability, and genuine choice. Power shapes possibility — it does not eliminate it.
