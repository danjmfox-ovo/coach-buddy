# Teams: Team Topologies and Psychological Safety

## Team Topologies (Skelton, Pais)

**What it is**: A model for organising teams and their interactions to optimise for flow of value. Grounded in Conway's Law.

**Source**: Skelton, M. & Pais, M. (2019). *Team Topologies: Organising Business and Technology Teams for Fast Flow*. IT Revolution Press.

---

### Four team types

| Type | Purpose | Ownership style |
|------|---------|----------------|
| **Stream-aligned** | Deliver value in a stream of work aligned to a business domain | Full ownership of a product or service area |
| **Platform** | Enable stream-aligned teams via self-service capabilities | Owns shared infrastructure, reduces cognitive load |
| **Enabling** | Help stream-aligned teams acquire capabilities they don't have | Temporary; coaches, then steps back |
| **Complicated Subsystem** | Handle areas requiring deep specialist knowledge | Owns components too complex for a stream-aligned team |

**The trap to name**: Most organisations have too many "complicated subsystem" teams that have become gatekeepers. Stream-aligned teams wait on them, flow breaks, everyone is frustrated.

---

### Three interaction modes

| Mode | Description | When it's right |
|------|-------------|----------------|
| **Collaboration** | Work closely together, high bandwidth, short-term | Discovering new things, building something novel |
| **X-as-a-Service** | One team consumes another's output with minimal interaction | Stable interfaces, predictable needs |
| **Facilitating** | Enabling team helps stream-aligned team grow capability | Deliberate upskilling; enabling team exits when capability is established |

**The diagnostic question**: What interaction mode is this team trying to operate in, and what mode are they actually in? Mismatches cause friction that looks like "personality conflict" or "poor communication."

---

### Conway's Law

> "Organisations which design systems are constrained to produce designs which are copies of the communication structures of those organisations." — Melvin Conway, 1968

**Inverse Conway manoeuvre**: Design the team structure you want, and the architecture will follow. Don't design the architecture first and hope the teams align.

**Coaching use**: When a coach's client is frustrated that their system architecture doesn't match their intentions — ask about the team structure. Usually the architecture is a mirror.

---

### Cognitive load

Team Topologies draws on Cognitive Load Theory (Sweller) to frame team size and scope. Two key types:

| Type | Description | What it produces |
|------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Intrinsic** | The complexity of the work itself | Unavoidable; manage by team expertise |
| **Extraneous** | Complexity added by process, tooling, coordination, context-switching | Avoidable; reduce ruthlessly |
| **Germane** | Load that builds capability and understanding | Invest in this |

**The diagnostic**: Is the team's cognitive load dominated by intrinsic complexity (right-size the team, deepen expertise) or extraneous complexity (remove friction, reduce dependencies, simplify interfaces)?

## Psychological Safety (Edmondson)

**What it is**: A shared belief within a team that it is safe to take interpersonal risks — to speak up, ask questions, admit mistakes, propose ideas — without fear of punishment or humiliation.

**Source**: Edmondson, A. (1999). "Psychological Safety and Learning Behavior in Work Teams." *Administrative Science Quarterly*. Also: Edmondson, A. (2018). *The Fearless Organisation*. Wiley.

---

### Critical distinctions

**Psychological safety is not**:
- Comfort — teams with high psychological safety still have hard conversations
- Agreement — it enables productive disagreement, not consensus
- Low standards — the research shows high standards + high safety = highest performance (the "learning zone")

**The 2x2**:

|  | Low standards | High standards |
|--|--------------|----------------|
| **High safety** | Comfort zone | Learning zone (the goal) |
| **Low safety** | Apathy zone | Anxiety zone |

Most teams that leaders describe as "low energy" or "people aren't engaged" are in the comfort zone (low standards) or apathy zone. High safety alone is not the goal.

---

### What undermines it

- Public blame or humiliation after failure (even subtle: eye-rolls, sighs)
- Questions treated as challenges to authority
- Bad news going unreported because the messenger gets shot
- "Brilliant jerk" culture — high performance tolerated despite interpersonal damage
- Inconsistency: safe to speak up for some people, not others

---

### Coaching applications

- Team retros where nobody says anything hard: safety problem, not engagement problem
- Leaders who say "my door is always open" but whose team never raises problems: safety problem
- Teams that ship without flagging risks: safety + standards problem
- Psychological safety as a prerequisite for learning, not a nice-to-have: name this explicitly when a coach is trying to build capability in a low-trust environment
