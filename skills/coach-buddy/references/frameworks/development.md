# Development: Kegan's Orders of Mind and Vertical Development

## Kegan's Orders of Mind (Kegan)

**What it is**: A theory of adult psychological development describing qualitatively different ways of making meaning — not what people know, but how they know it. Later stages can hold more complexity, contradiction, and ambiguity.

**Source**: Kegan, R. (1982). *The Evolving Self*. Harvard University Press.
Kegan, R. & Lahey, L. (2009). *Immunity to Change*. Harvard Business Review Press.

---

### The orders relevant to organisational coaching

| Order | Name | How meaning is made | Limit |
|-------|------|-------------------|-------|
| **3** | Socialised Mind | Through others' expectations, relationships, group membership | Can't hold own view against the group; struggles with genuine conflict |
| **4** | Self-Authoring Mind | Through own framework, values, ideology | Can hold own view; can lead; struggles when own framework is the problem |
| **5** | Self-Transforming Mind | Through awareness of own framework as one perspective among many | Comfortable with ambiguity; can hold multiple frameworks without needing to resolve them |

**Note**: Most adults operate predominantly at Order 3 or 4. Order 5 is rare and not the "goal" — each order is appropriate for different contexts and roles.

---

### The "developmental gap"

A role's demands often exceed what the current order of mind can hold:

- An Order 3 leader in a senior role that requires holding own convictions against team pressure: **developmental mismatch**
- An Order 4 expert whose framework has stopped working but who can't question it from inside: **developmental ceiling**

This is not a deficit — it is a description of a growth edge. The coaching question is: what would support the person in developing the next capacity? Not: how do we get them to Order 5.

---

### Immunity to Change (Kegan & Lahey)

A practical application of Kegan's theory. People who genuinely want to change and can't are often running an **immune system** — a competing commitment that protects a hidden assumption.

**The four-column map**:
1. Improvement goal (what I say I want)
2. What I'm doing instead (behaviour that undermines the goal)
3. Competing commitment (what the behaviour is protecting)
4. Big assumption (the belief that makes the protection feel necessary)

**Example**:
- Goal: Give clearer feedback to my team
- Doing instead: Softening feedback, hedging, avoiding hard conversations
- Competing commitment: Protecting relationships, being seen as supportive
- Big assumption: "If I give hard feedback, people will disengage or see me as aggressive"

The immunity is not irrational — it makes perfect sense given the assumption. The work is testing the assumption, not willpowering past the behaviour.

---

### When to reach for this

- Coach is working with someone who keeps failing to follow through on the same type of commitment
- A leader knows what good looks like but can't seem to do it consistently
- A change initiative is blocked despite genuine buy-in — the blockage is individual, not systemic
- Someone is in a role that their current meaning-making can't yet hold

**What it is not for**: Diagnosing someone else's stage. Kegan's theory is best used as a frame for understanding stuck patterns, not as a label for people.

---

### Vertical vs Horizontal Development

**Horizontal development**: acquiring new skills, knowledge, behaviours within the current order of mind. Most training and coaching operates here.

**Vertical development**: a shift in the order of mind itself — how meaning is made, not just what is known.

The distinction matters when a coach is trying to understand why training and skill-building aren't producing the change a leader or team needs. If the constraint is meaning-making, horizontal development won't reach it.
