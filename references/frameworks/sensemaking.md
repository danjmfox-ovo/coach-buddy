# Sensemaking: Weick

## Sensemaking (Weick)

**What it is**: A theory of how people construct meaning from ambiguous, equivocal situations — particularly in organisations. Sensemaking is retrospective: we understand what we were doing only after we act. It is social, ongoing, and identity-driven.

**Source**: Weick, K. (1979). *The Social Psychology of Organising*. Addison-Wesley.
Weick, K. (1995). *Sensemaking in Organisations*. Sage.
Weick, K., Sutcliffe, K. & Obstfeld, D. (2005). "Organising and the Process of Sensemaking." *Organisation Science*.

---

### Seven properties of sensemaking

Weick identified seven properties that distinguish sensemaking from other cognitive processes:

| Property | What it means |
|----------|--------------|
| **Grounded in identity** | We make sense relative to who we believe we are. Threats to identity disrupt sensemaking. |
| **Retrospective** | Meaning is made after action, not before. Plans are rationalised in hindsight. |
| **Enactive** | We create the environment we then respond to. Action produces the cues we interpret. |
| **Social** | Sensemaking is always in relation to others — actual, imagined, or implied. |
| **Ongoing** | It never stops. We are always in the middle of it. |
| **Focused on extracted cues** | We notice particular signals and build meaning from them, ignoring most of the environment. |
| **Driven by plausibility, not accuracy** | "Does this hang together well enough to act?" not "Is this correct?" |

**The coaching implication**: When a team or leader is "stuck," they are often holding an account of the situation that felt plausible when it formed but has since become a constraint. The account is no longer being updated because new cues are being filtered through the old story.

---

### Narrative and account-giving

For Weick, the organisation is a network of interlocking accounts — stories people tell about why things are the way they are. When disruption occurs (a project fails, a leader leaves, a strategy changes), the shared account breaks down and people experience **cosmological episodes**: moments when the sense they have made of the world suddenly doesn't hold.

**What a cosmological episode looks like**: confusion, paralysis, a sense that "nothing makes sense anymore," withdrawal, or frantic over-activity. It is not irrationality — it is the absence of a usable account.

**The coaching move**: Not "what should we do?" but "what account of this situation is the team holding?" and "what new account would they need to act?"

---

### Equivocality vs. uncertainty

Weick distinguishes two different information problems:

| Problem | Definition | Response |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| **Uncertainty** | Not enough information | Gather more data |
| **Equivocality** | Too many possible interpretations | Reduce interpretations through conversation |

**The common error**: treating equivocality as uncertainty. Organisations respond to ambiguous situations by commissioning more research, running more surveys, building bigger dashboards. The problem is not lack of data — it is that the data could mean too many things. The response to equivocality is dialogue, not data.

---

### The "how does this happen" question

One of Weick's most productive interventions is replacing "why did this happen?" with "how did this come to happen?" — a shift from attribution (finding a cause, often a person) to process (understanding the sequence of conditions that made this outcome possible). This is directly relevant to post-incident reviews, blameful retrospectives, and root-cause analysis that stalls in blame.

---

### When to reach for this

- A team or leader is describing the same situation in a way that has clearly stopped working, but they keep re-applying it
- A retrospective or debrief is producing blame rather than understanding
- A major change (restructure, strategy shift, leadership change) has left a team disoriented — they are "not themselves"
- A team keeps gathering more data but not converging on action
- A coach is noticing that the client's account of their situation is coherent but narrow — carefully explored boundaries, not tested

---

### Common misreads

- "Sensemaking" as a synonym for "making sense" (i.e. thinking clearly). In Weick's usage it is a specific, socially-embedded, retrospective process — not just careful reasoning.
- The retrospective property does not mean plans are useless. It means that the meaning of action is constructed after the fact. Plans are hypotheses about what will make sense in retrospect.
- Weick's work is descriptive before it is prescriptive. It describes what actually happens when people are in novel, ambiguous situations — not what should happen.
